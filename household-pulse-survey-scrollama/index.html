COVID-19, Anxiety and Depression

How a year of pandemic took a toll on Americans' mental health





As seen by the daily case count, COVID-19 came in ever larger waves.

In April 2020, the CDC partnered with the Census Bureau to track the pandemic's mental health impact.

The Household Pulse Survey researches rates of anxiety and depression among Americans, as well as their treatment and insurance circumstances.

The researchers found the results revealing.

Anxiety rates followed a strikingly similar trajectory as COVID-19.

When they measured depression, they found the same thing.

Depression rates tracked COVID-19 case counts, especially in the winter surge.

The virus's disproportionate impact on people of color also became apparent.

Not only were people of color and mixed race paying a steeper cost in deaths and economic impact, their mental health was also suffering unequally.

The CDC also found the pandemic's effects on mental health to differ by age.

Young people were the hardest hit.

But when the researchers looked at how many anxious and depressed Americans were getting treatment, the results were disturbing.

They found that many anxious and depression people weren't getting the treatment they needed.

Anxiety, Depression Climbed Amid Pandemic


Anxiety and depression leapt among Americans as the COVID-19 pandemic raged last fall and winter.

A new report from the Centers for Disease Control and Prevention shows the percentage of American adults with recent symptoms of an anxiety or depressive disorder jumped from 36.4% in August 2020 to 42.4% in February 2021.

The report summarizes the CDC and Census Bureau’s Household Pulse Survey, launched to rapidly monitor mental health status and access to care during the pandemic.


Hispanics, Blacks Suffered Disproportionately


Significant increases in symptoms of anxiety and depression were observed for all racial and ethnic groups except for non-Hispanic adults reporting races other than Black, White or Asian.

Hispanic or Latino Americans saw the greatest jump of 8.2 percentage points.

Blacks suffered the next worse increase of 7.2 percentage points in numbers reporting increased symptoms of anxiety or depression between August and February.

Asians saw a jump of 5 percentage points and whites experienced a rise of 2.7 percentage points.

Women, with a 4.4 percentage point rise, and men with a 4.3 percentage point rise seem to have been affected similarly over the time frame.


The Young Hit Hardest


Anxiety and depression rose significantly for all age groups except adults aged 80 and over.

The largest increases hit the young. The number of 18-29 year-olds reporting symptoms of anxiety or depression rose 8 percentage points from August 2020 to February 2021.

All demographic groups showed rises in anxiety and depression as COVID-19 cases jumped in the summer and fall.

The report’s authors note that, “The spread of disease and increase in deaths during large outbreaks of transmissible diseases is often associated with fear and grief.”

They say social restrictions and business closings can further increase the risk for mental health problems.

Between January 20 and February 1 this year, more than two in five adults aged 18 and over reported experiencing symptoms of an anxiety or depressive disorder in the past seven days.

Respondents with less than a high school education were also among the most affected, suffering a 7.8 percentage point increase in symptoms of an anxiety or depressive disorder in the past week.

By contrast, only a 2.2 percentage point rise was observed among Americans with a Bachelor’s degree or higher.

The Household Pulse survey began in April after the pandemic was already underway.

But the authors cite for context the roughly 11% rate for an anxiety and/or depressive disorder among respondents to the 2019 National Health Interview Survey.


Unmet Mental Health Needs


The number of Americans receiving prescription medications and mental health counseling saw a significant jump as COVID-19 cases rose from fall into winter–but the increase in unmet mental health needs was even higher.

From August to February, the percentage of Americans receiving mental health care treatment increased from 22.4% to 24.8% for a 2.4 percentage point increase.

But in the same period, the number of Americans with symptoms of an anxiety or a depressive disorder and unmet mental health needs rose 2.8 percentage points from 21% to 23.8%.

Young adults and those with less than a high school education were again the worst affected.

The number of adults aged 18-29 saw an increase of 7.2 percentage points in unmet mental health needs, while those with less than a high school education experienced a 4.3 point rise.
